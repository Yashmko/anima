Anima — design bible

The visual and emotional soul of the world

Design summary

Anima is a life RPG where real consistency reshapes a small, sacred, pixel-art world. The aesthetic owes more to a half-remembered storybook than to a SaaS dashboard: cherry blossom valleys, moss-softened ruins, lantern-lit paths, and a protagonist who is gently small against a world that visibly remembers them. Every design decision should answer one question — does this make someone want to stay? Color stays warm and quiet. Pixels stay deliberate, never decorative for its own sake. UI stays out of the world's way. Motion stays as gentle as breathing. The result should feel discovered, not manufactured — a place, not a product.

1. Brand philosophy

Anima's brand promise is not "build better habits faster." It's slower and stranger than that: real life is imperfect and unhurried, and Anima doesn't try to fix that — it honors it by turning small, repeated, ordinary acts into a world that visibly remembers them. The brand should never feel like it's selling self-improvement. It should feel like it's keeping a place for the player, the way a forest keeps growing whether or not anyone is watching.

The defining brand discipline is restraint. What Anima refuses to do — manipulate attention, rush the player, flood the screen, perform urgency — is as important to its identity as what it does. Every other life-RPG and habit app competes on engagement. Anima competes on the feeling of having somewhere quiet to return to.

2. Emotional goals

Seven feelings define the experience. Each needs a concrete design behavior behind it, not just a mood word.

Cozy — achieved through scale, light temperature, and texture, never through clutter. A sheltered, enclosed space (a porch, a tea house, a hollow beneath a willow) reads cozier than an open plain, regardless of color.

Peaceful — an absence of urgency in both pacing and interface. Nothing on screen competes for attention at once; the eye always has somewhere quiet to land.

Magical — implied, never announced. A softly glowing mushroom, a path that wasn't there yesterday, a spirit glimpsed only at the edge of the frame. Magic that explains itself with a VFX burst stops feeling magical.

Nostalgic — nostalgia for a half-remembered place, not a pixel-perfect retro homage. It should feel like a dream of a game you played as a child, not a museum recreation of one.

Hopeful — shown through growth visible in the world (a sapling becomes a tree, a ruin gains a new lantern) rather than told through dialogue or progress bars.

Intimate — the world is made for one person at a time. Even social or comparative features, if they exist, should never make Anima feel crowded or performative.

Quietly adventurous — adventure at walking pace. The thrill is discovery, not danger; the stakes are emotional, not mechanical.

3. Artistic principles

The world is the hero. Any frame should work as a postcard from the world even with the player and UI removed.

Restraint is a feature. The instinct to add one more detail, one more particle, one more color is the instinct to resist by default.

Silhouette before detail. If a shape doesn't read at thumbnail size, it doesn't belong in Anima, however nice the close-up detail is.

History over invention. Everything in the world looks like it has a past — moss, wear, repair, weather — rather than looking freshly generated.

Discovery over instruction. The world teaches itself through environmental storytelling. Tutorial UI is the last resort, never the first instinct.

These principles sit closer to the unhurried wonder of a Miyazaki forest or the lived-in stillness of games like A Short Hike or Spiritfarer than to anything built for a pitch deck. Use those as tonal compasses, never as templates to copy.

4. Pixel art principles

Medium pixel density. Assets should sit closer to a SNES-era JRPG feel than either ultra-fine 8-bit micro-detail or AI-upscaled mush. Pixels are a deliberate, visible choice — never disguised, never smoothed away.

One light source per scene, with soft two- to three-step dithering for gradients instead of harsh banding or smooth gradients that fight the pixel medium.

Hand-placed feel. Even AI-assisted generation must be art-directed and cleaned by a human eye for tile alignment, repeat patterns, and silhouette clarity. Nothing should look auto-generated.

Palette discipline per scene. Eight to twelve dominant colors drawn from the master palette below, never the full palette splashed across one screen.

Watch for "AI sludge" tells: muddy color blending, inconsistent pixel grid, illegible micro-detail, melting edges. A tile that impresses zoomed in but reads as noise zoomed out has failed.

5. Composition rules

Negative space bias. At least 30–40% of any establishing frame should be open sky, water, or unbroken ground. Breathing room is not wasted space — it is the cozy.

Three-layer depth. Foreground silhouette (branches, grass, a stone edge) / midground action (player, paths, structures) / background atmosphere (distant mountains, soft haze, sky). Every explorable scene needs all three.

Small protagonist ratio. In wide or establishing shots, the player character occupies roughly 3–6% of frame height — large enough to anchor empathy, small enough that the world still dominates.

Leading lines. Paths, rivers, bridges, and light pull the eye toward an exit point or point of interest. No frame should be a dead end with nowhere for the eye to travel.

Corner details. At least one small, easy-to-miss detail per scene — a sleeping cat, a hidden lantern, a carved symbol — rewards close looking without ever demanding it.

6. Camera angle recommendations

The primary camera is a gentle three-quarter top-down angle, slightly elevated — close enough to classic JRPG framing to read paths and depth clearly, without flattening into an overhead map.

For cinematic or establishing moments (entering a new region, a major story beat), use slow lateral pans or soft push-ins. Never hard cuts, whip pans, or anything borrowed from action-game cinematography — no dramatic low-angle hero shots, no Dutch tilts, no camera shake. The camera in Anima is a patient observer, not a director chasing the action, and it should rarely move faster than the player can comfortably read the scene.

7. Animation philosophy

Ambient motion is the backbone of the world feeling alive: drifting petals, swaying lanterns, rippling water, blinking fireflies, gliding birds, grass responding faintly to wind. These run continuously and quietly enough that their loop point is never obvious within a normal viewing window.

Character motion stays grounded and soft — idle animations show small life (a glance, a breath, a stretch) rather than power poses. Transitions are dissolves and fades, never hard cuts; a loading moment should feel like a page turning, not a spinner. UI motion follows the same law: slow ease-in-out easing, no bounce, no elastic overshoot, no particle spam, no screen shake.

A useful test: mute all motion. If the scene still feels alive because of color and light, the animation is doing its proper, additive job. If the scene feels dead without it, too much storytelling weight is resting on motion that should be resting on art direction.

10. UI principles

UI is a guest in the world, not a layer on top of it. Translucent panels are preferred over solid opaque cards wherever legibility allows, and every surface gets generous padding — UI density should never rival the density of the art beneath it. Icons are simple outlined linework, never bold filled glyphs competing with the pixel style.

Notifications and badges stay quiet: a soft glow or small dot, never a loud counter demanding attention. Color in UI is reserved for genuine state changes — an achievement, a newly unlocked area — not spread decoratively across every button and tab. Motion in UI mirrors the world's motion philosophy exactly: soft easing, no bounce, no aggressive micro-interactions.

11. Environment motifs

Flora — cherry blossom forests, willow trees, bamboo groves, moss-covered stone, drifting petals.

Water — waterfalls, streams, ponds, gentle ripples.

Structures — marble ruins, wooden bridges, shrines, hidden libraries, tiny villages, mountain paths.

Light and atmosphere — lanterns, golden-hour lighting, fireflies, campfires.

Life — birds, butterflies, cats, subtle spirits glimpsed rather than shown directly.

Never combine more than two or three of these clusters in a single scene — overcrowding is the fastest way to break the feeling of breathing room. Every scene should reserve at least one area of genuinely uninterrupted negative space, even when the motif palette is rich.

12. Character design philosophy

The player's avatar must read instantly in silhouette at small scale, since most shots place them small within a large world — this rules out hyper-detailed armor, busy gear, or fine ornamentation that only resolves up close. Growth and progress should be shown primarily through subtle change in the world rather than a loud character glow-up; the character earning a new cloak color matters less than the path they walk gaining a new lantern.

Companions and spirits should feel like quiet animal-spirit hybrids — closer to a fox that's slightly more than a fox than to a generic fantasy familiar. Where a character's appearance does shift with player progress, the change should be subtle and seasonal (a softer palette, a new accessory) rather than a power-fantasy transformation.

13. Landing page philosophy

The homepage should not feel like marketing — it should feel like an entrance. No hero banner shouting a bold call-to-action; instead, a scroll-driven walk into the world, the way a path leads somewhere before you can see the destination. Copy reads like a found journal or a letter left for the player, not a sales pitch. The single goal of every word and frame is the feeling: I want to stay here.

Calls to action are framed as invitations — "step inside," not "get started free." If social proof appears at all, it should read like traveler's notes left in the margins, never a corporate review carousel.

14. Mobile-first guidance

Single-column, scroll-friendly environments that don't depend on hover states or precise cursor targeting.
Touch targets sized generously; nothing should require precision a thumb can't comfortably provide
Ambient animation scales down gracefully on mobile — replace heavy parallax layers with lighter alternatives rather than disabling motion entirely.
UI chrome shrinks further on small screens; let the world fill the frame edge to edge rather than competing with navigation bars.
Navigation is bottom-anchored and minimal, not a top-heavy menu stack.
Test type scaling at the smallest supported screen size — the storybook feeling breaks first at the typography layer.

15. Things we will never do

Never use neon, harsh saturation, or a dark dashboard palette.
Never simulate urgency — no countdown timers, no guilt-framed push notifications, no FOMO theater.
Never let hyper-detailed or "AI sludge" textures into the pixel art.
Never crowd a scene past comfortable density; if in doubt, remove rather than add.
Never let UI chrome carry more visual weight than the world it sits on.
Never default to generic fantasy tropes — the armored hero, the floating loot orb, the glowing exclamation mark.
Never make the player feel small in a belittling way — only ever spatially small within something larger and kinder.
Never use harsh cuts, screen shake, or particle spam.
Never reproduce real branded IP or visibly lift assets from an existing game.

17. North star statement

Anima is the feeling of returning to a place that remembers you — a small, sunlit valley that grows a little more whole every time you do something small and true in your own life, asking for nothing louder than your continued presence and offering nothing flashier than the quiet, accumulating proof that you were here, and that here is better for it.

Future-proofing note

As Anima grows — new biomes, new companions, seasonal events, new continents — every addition should pass one test before it ships: would this still feel discovered, not manufactured? A new region should feel like it was always there and the player simply hadn't found it yet, not like a content drop with a patch-notes voice. New systems should expand what the world can remember about the player, not what the UI can demand from them. The identity of Anima isn't a fixed set of assets — it's this discipline, reapplied every time something new is built.
